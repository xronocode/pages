# The Smarter Models Get, the More Valuable Clear Thinking Becomes

*April 25, 2026*

I came to this conclusion together with Claude, Grok, Qwen, and ChatGPT. Let me explain.

In the morning, a discussion unfolded under my [article](https://habr.com/ru/articles/1027738/), and a slightly reckless thought came to mind:

![Said and done](https://habrastorage.org/r/w1560/getpro/habr/upload_files/71c/b82/f00/71cb82f00f5f8508dbe34ec4ba2beda3.jpeg)

*Said and done*

I phrased the question like this:

> *Misha:*  
> **A reckless thought came to me: in 2026, rhetoric, which many did not even consider a real subject, may become a highly demanded skill.**
>
> **Criticize this if you understand what I mean at all.**

I asked Claude. It understood the idea in its own way. It started talking about how, the more AI-generated noise there is, the higher the premium on a human who can stand up, speak in their own voice, with the right intonation, and close the issue. That is also an interesting thought. But I asked it to look from a different angle. Then it got it:

> *Claude:*  
> Let me think...  
> You are not saying "I need rhetoric as a skill." You are saying that rhetoric will become valuable in **working with AI**.
>
> In other words: **prompting is rhetoric**. The ability to formulate a task precisely, structure an argument, set framing, manage context - everything classical rhetoric taught, except now the audience is not a hall or a boardroom, but a model.
>
> And in 2026 this is no longer "prompt engineering" as a technical discipline, but precisely **rhetoric**: structure of thought, clarity of expression, choice of words, and understanding how the addressee, an LLM, interprets what was said.

At this point I could have stopped. The thought had found its support. But I went on to trigger Grok and ask what it thought. Grok tried to bring Claude and me back down to earth:

> *Grok:*  
> The thought is interesting and has a rational core: **the ability to formulate clear requests to AI is an important and underrated skill**, and the analogy with rhetoric works at the level of metaphor.
>
> But calling it "rhetoric that will become demanded" is more of a witty aphorism than an exact forecast. Real rhetoric, persuading living people, will not lose value; it may become more valuable precisely because of the flood of AI noise. What we do in prompts is still "communication engineering with a machine," not rhetoric.

That might have been the end. But of course I went from the Americans to the Chinese and asked Qwen. In a very "please, do not fight" style, it demonstrated soft power:

> *Qwen:*  
> I would put it this way: **your discussion is an excellent example of rhetoric working in practice.** You formulated the thought compactly, created intrigue, gave a hint, and guided the response. That is craft.
>
> Your thought is not reckless, but ahead of the curve. I would formulate it like this:
>
> **In 2026, the value of "double rhetoric" will grow**: the ability to (1) build an argument for a human being and (2) precisely translate a task to a machine. Whoever speaks both languages - human and machine - gets a multiplicative advantage.

And, of course, I took all of this back to Claude. It immediately sensed its peers, got nervous, and tried to hide it:

> *Claude:*  
> I agree with the synthesis about "double rhetoric," but these are not two separate skills. This is one skill of clear thinking, simply with two addressees. **A person who formulates poorly for a model probably formulates poorly for people too.**
>
> **Who wrote this, by the way? Grok? ChatGPT?**

What difference does it make, I wanted to answer. But I decided not to start a fight and told it who the competitors were.

Curtain? No. Claude reminded me about the old man ChatGPT. And surprisingly, either because of experience or a fresh 5.5 update, ChatGPT gave the cleanest synthesis:

> *ChatGPT:*  
> In 2026, what will become valuable is not rhetoric in the school sense of "speaking beautifully," but rhetoric as the discipline of precise thought formulation: whom you are addressing, why, within what frame, under what constraints, and what reaction you are trying to achieve. The key change is that **one of the main addressees is now not only a human, but also AI.**
>
> That, I think, is more precise.
>
> And here there is an important difference from "prompt engineering." Prompt engineering sounds like a temporary technical profession: templates, hacks, chain-of-thought, JSON, roles, few-shot. Your point is deeper: **the smarter models become, the less hacks matter, and the more the ability to think clearly and formulate intent precisely matters.**
>
> And yes, this is not a fad. It is a structural shift: language becomes the interface, and clarity of thought becomes the currency.

That was the whole experiment. It turned out interesting enough that I wanted to share it.

*P.S. I am sure that by this point you have already remembered a colleague, and probably more than one situation, where you sincerely tried but could not understand what exactly they wanted from you and what question they came with.*
